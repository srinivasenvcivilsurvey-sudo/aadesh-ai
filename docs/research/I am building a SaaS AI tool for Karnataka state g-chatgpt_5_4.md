# I am building a SaaS AI tool for Karnataka state government land revenue

offices (DDLR, ADLR, AC, DC, Tahsildar offices). The tool helps individual
caseworkers draft legal orders using AI.

Research these specific questions for Karnataka, India as of 2026:

1. Can individual government officers purchase SaaS software tools for
their office use? Or does it HAVE to go through department-level
procurement?
2. What is the Government e-Marketplace (GeM) registration process for
a software SaaS startup? Is GeM registration mandatory to sell to
Karnataka state government?
3. What certifications or approvals does a startup need to sell AI
software to Indian government offices? (STQC, MeitY empanelment,
DPIIT Startup recognition, etc.)
4. How does Karnataka's IT department (headed by Minister Priyank Kharge)
currently procure AI/software tools? Any recent examples?
5. Is there any existing AI tool or digital system currently used in
Karnataka land revenue offices (DDLR/Tahsildar) for document drafting?
6. What is KSWAN (Karnataka State Wide Area Network) and does our SaaS
tool need to work within KSWAN infrastructure?
7. Karnataka formed a Responsible AI Committee (March 2026, chaired by
Kris Gopalakrishnan). What procurement guidelines might this
committee recommend for AI tools used in government?
8. What is the typical budget range that a Tahsildar or DDLR office has
for purchasing software tools per year?
9. Can a startup sell directly to officers informally (officer pays from
pocket) before getting formal government procurement approval?
10. Any examples of Indian startups that successfully sold SaaS tools
to state-level revenue/land offices? What was their approach?

Give specific, actionable answers with sources where possible.
Focus on Karnataka state specifically, not just India generally.

For Karnataka, you should assume that any paid, official use of your SaaS in a government office must be routed through the office or department as the procurement entity, not through an individual officer’s personal subscription, though Karnataka’s procurement rules do leave room for low-value exceptions and startup-friendly technology procurement paths.  GeM matters and is worth setting up early, but the stronger Karnataka strategy is GeM readiness plus KTPP compliance plus a department-approved pilot that fits the state’s current centralized AI modernization push.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5]

[^3] Karnataka’s updated rules also show a startup-specific procurement route for technology products/goods/solutions up to ₹50 lakh through the Karnataka Public Procurement Portal, but that route still runs through the procurement entity rather than a caseworker buying on their own. [^4] In the Karnataka materials reviewed, I found KTPP and the Karnataka Public Procurement Portal as the core state procurement framework, but I did not find a Karnataka-wide rule in these sources saying every state software purchase must go through GeM only. ## What to do next

Your best near-term route is: register as a GeM seller/service provider, get Startup India recognition if you qualify, prepare a government-ready security pack, and structure your first deployment as a written pilot rather than an informal paid subscription.  In Karnataka specifically, also position the product as a departmental productivity and legal-consistency tool that can fit into the state’s centralized AI modernization agenda around land-stack, Kaveri 3.0, and EITBT-led AI programs.[^2][^6][^4][^5][^7]

A practical first-sales sequence would be: 1) secure one written pilot note from a Tahsildar/DDLR/ADLR office, 2) keep the pilot scope narrow to drafting assistance with human approval, 3) document time saved and drafting consistency, and 4) convert that into a formal purchase routed through the office or a higher department using Karnataka’s procurement process.  If your annual contract value can stay within the startup-friendly technology procurement band referenced in Karnataka’s updated rules, that improves your odds of getting an early departmental order without a large conventional tender.[^3][^1]

## Procurement checklist

- Use Indian hosting and prepare a short security note, because government buyers will ask where data is stored and how access is controlled, especially as Karnataka develops its responsible AI framework.[^9][^10][^7]
- Build audit logs and mandatory human review into every draft, because Karnataka’s AI policy direction is explicitly about safe and transparent AI in citizen-affecting systems.[^10][^9]
- Make the app lightweight and browser-based, because departments may access it over KSWAN-managed connectivity rather than open consumer-style internet setups.[^8]
- Pitch both the user department and the state tech side, because Karnataka’s recent AI/software initiatives are being driven through centralized state programs, not only office-level purchases.[^5][^2]
