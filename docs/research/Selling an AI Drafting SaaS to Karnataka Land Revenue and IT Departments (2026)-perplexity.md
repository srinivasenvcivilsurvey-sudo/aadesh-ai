# Selling an AI Drafting SaaS to Karnataka Land Revenue and IT Departments (2026)

## Executive summary

Karnataka’s land revenue ecosystem is already heavily digitised through Bhoomi, Kaveri, Mojini, e‑Swathu/e‑Asthi and is now moving into an AI‑enabled “Land‑Stack” architecture with Kaveri 3.0 and Bhoomi 8.0. Procurement of new software and AI tools is governed mainly by the Karnataka Transparency in Public Procurement (KTPP) Act and Rules, the Karnataka Financial Code, and (for cloud) MeitY’s MeghRaj/Cloud guidelines and GeM. Individual officers have limited delegated financial powers for contingent expenditure but cannot bypass these frameworks to procure official SaaS tools using personal payments. MeitY’s policy on use of IT resources and cloud guidelines, plus emerging India‑wide AI governance norms and Karnataka’s new Responsible AI Committee, all point towards controlled, risk‑based adoption of AI with strong security, data‑localisation and human‑oversight requirements.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6][^7][^8][^9]

For your AI order‑drafting SaaS, the practical route is: (a) host on a MeitY‑empanelled cloud (or inside the State Data Centre) reachable over KSWAN; (b) register on GeM, ideally under the Startup Runway with DPIIT recognition; (c) align with forthcoming Responsible‑AI norms and existing GoI AI governance guidelines; and (d) sell via pilots and formal RFPs led by the Revenue Department, IT/BT Department or CeG, rather than informal officer‑level purchases.[^10][^11][^12]

***

## 1. Can individual officers buy SaaS, or must procurement be departmental?

### 1.1 Legal framework in Karnataka

Public procurement by Karnataka departments is governed by the Karnataka Transparency in Public Procurement (KTPP) Act 1999 and its Rules, which make transparent, competitive procedures mandatory for procurement of goods and services by “procurement entities” of the State. The Finance Department maintains updated KTPP amendments and standardised tender documents, confirming that departments and notified entities – not individual officers – are the units expected to run tenders and sign contracts.[^13][^2][^3][^14][^15]

In parallel, Karnataka follows the Karnataka Financial Code and contingent‑expenditure rules, which define how heads of offices can incur office expenses and contingent charges within sanctioned budget grants. These rules allow heads of offices some discretion for small “contingencies”, but they explicitly tie all such expenditure to approved budget heads and bar any spending beyond budget grants without Government sanction.[^16][^17]

### 1.2 Implications for SaaS procurement

Nothing in the KTPP Act or Karnataka Financial Code creates a carve‑out that would allow an individual officer to unilaterally purchase recurring software or SaaS subscriptions for official use outside the formal procurement system. Instead, a Tahsildar, DDLR or similar authority can exercise only the delegated financial powers and procurement routes prescribed for their office (e.g., small‑value contingencies, or acting as the “procuring entity” under KTPP and Finance‑Department orders).[^2][^16]

At all higher values, or where contracts involve cloud hosting, data processing or multi‑year commitments, procurement is expected to be conducted by the concerned department (Revenue, IT/BT, CeG, etc.) under KTPP via e‑procurement and/or GeM, not as an individual subscription purchased with a government credit card.[^18][^2]

**Actionable takeaway:** design your go‑to‑market around departmental or directorate‑level procurement (Revenue Dept, Survey Settlement & Land Records, Registration & Stamps, or IT/BT Department), not around selling subscriptions directly to individual officers for official use.

## 2. GeM registration for a SaaS startup and whether it is mandatory

### 2.1 What GeM is and who uses it

Government e‑Marketplace (GeM) is the national public procurement portal for goods and services for all Central and State Government ministries, departments, and PSUs. Official descriptions emphasise that GeM is the central, unified platform to procure common‑use goods and services, and that government buyer accounts are created at the level of HoDs or primary users in each organisation.[^19][^20]

The Bengaluru Urban district site explicitly points Karnataka departments to GeM as the one‑stop platform for online procurement of common‑use goods and services, referencing Rule 149 of the General Financial Rules (GFR) 2017. Central finance instructions make procurement of goods and services that are available on GeM mandatory for Central ministries and departments, with any off‑GeM procurement requiring a “GeM Availability Report and Past Transaction Summary” justification.[^21][^22]

### 2.2 Seller/SaaS registration process

The official services portal for India summarises GeM seller registration as: (1) visit the GeM portal; (2) choose “Seller/Service Provider”; (3) complete online registration with business details; and (4) list products/services to participate in tenders. Third‑party guides focused on Karnataka sellers list the typical documents: PAN, Aadhaar, GST registration, bank details, incorporation documents, and (optionally) MSME/Udyam certificates.[^23][^19]

For startups, the “Startup Runway” on GeM allows DPIIT‑recognised startups to list innovative products, often without prior turnover or experience requirements, and provides relaxed conditions such as exemption from earnest money and flexibility on experience criteria. Startup‑focused write‑ups emphasise that DPIIT recognition is an important enabler for these benefits and for easier participation in government tenders through GeM.[^24][^12][^25]

### 2.3 Is GeM mandatory to sell to Karnataka government?

The Government of India classifies GeM as the procurement portal for both Central and State Government buyers, and states are increasingly routing standardised purchases through it. Karnataka issues procurement through both the state e‑procurement portal and GeM; there is a growing volume of Karnataka‑origin tenders on GeM across departments.[^26][^20][^18]

GeM registration is **not legally mandatory** for all businesses in Karnataka, but any business that wants to sell to government departments or PSUs is generally expected to be on GeM, particularly when the relevant service category exists on the portal. In practice, many IT and cloud services tenders either (a) require that the service be offered via GeM, or (b) require at least a GeM availability report before an off‑portal tender is justified.[^5][^22][^23]

**Actionable takeaway:** in your case, GeM registration as a service provider is strongly recommended and will likely be practically necessary to participate in most standardised software/AI procurements by Karnataka departments.

## 3. Certifications and approvals needed to sell AI software to government

### 3.1 MeitY cloud empanelment and STQC for cloud infrastructure

Under the MeghRaj/“GI Cloud” initiative, MeitY empanels cloud service offerings of Cloud Service Providers (CSPs) for three deployment models (Public Cloud, Virtual Private Cloud, Government Community Cloud) and three service models (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS). Empanelment requires CSPs to undergo security and compliance assessment, including audits by STQC (Standardisation Testing and Quality Certification) of their data centres and cloud service offerings.[^11][^10][^5]

MeitY’s cloud procurement guidelines explicitly state that government departments should procure cloud services (including SaaS) from MeitY‑empanelled CSPs via GeM, and that SaaS offerings should run from data centres audited and qualified under this empanelment process. Empanelled CSPs are required to comply with standards such as ISO 27001, ISO 27017, ISO 27018 and ISO 20000‑1, and undergo periodic surveillance audits.[^4][^10][^11]

### 3.2 Application‑level STQC, GIGW and QA frameworks

STQC itself is a certification and testing directorate of MeitY that provides quality and security certification for electronics, IT products and e‑government systems. STQC certification is **mandatory only where specified in laws, guidelines or programme conditions** – for example, Aadhaar devices, CCTV procured under the 2024 Public Procurement Order, and GIGW compliance certification for government websites.[^27][^28][^29][^30]

For e‑governance applications, the GoI Quality Assurance Framework and Conformity Assessment Requirements (CARE) recommend third‑party quality and security assessment through empanelled bodies such as STQC, with the choice depending on risk, project scale and RFP requirements. In practice, many large e‑governance projects require STQC testing or similar third‑party audits at least for security and performance, even if not mandated by statute.[^31][^32][^30]

### 3.3 DPIIT Startup recognition and procurement benefits

DPIIT Startup Recognition, under Startup India, provides benefits such as income‑tax exemptions, IPR fee rebates, and importantly easier access to government tenders. Recognised startups get exemptions from prior turnover and experience criteria in certain tenders and can participate in the GeM Startup Runway programme, which has enabled thousands of startups to fulfil large government orders.[^12][^25][^33][^24]

### 3.4 AI‑specific governance guidelines

MeitY’s IndiaAI Governance Guidelines (2025) propose an AI governance framework with principles such as risk‑based classification of AI systems, whole‑of‑government coordination, sectoral regulators leading contextual governance, and strengthened grievance‑redressal and oversight institutions. These guidelines emphasise using existing laws and sectoral regulations as the first layer, with additional controls for higher‑risk AI use‑cases.[^34][^35]

Although these are governance guidelines rather than a certification scheme, they are likely to influence how RFPs for AI systems are written (e.g., requirements for risk classification, logging, explainability and human‑in‑the‑loop controls).

### 3.5 Practical stack for your SaaS

For an AI drafting SaaS aimed at government:

- **Underlying cloud:** host on a MeitY‑empanelled CSP (AWS India, Azure India, NIC, etc.), and reference the CSP’s MeitY/STQC status in proposals.[^10][^11]
- **Security posture:** align with GIGW 3.0 and the Quality Assurance Framework; be prepared to undergo third‑party security audits (potentially STQC) if the RFP demands it.[^28][^31]
- **Startup positioning:** obtain DPIIT Startup Recognition to leverage GeM Startup Runway benefits and relaxed procurement criteria.[^25][^12]
- **AI governance:** map your system to the IndiaAI Governance Guidelines’ risk categories, and document safeguards (human review, audit logs, bias controls) for high‑impact decisions such as quasi‑judicial orders.[^35][^34]

## 4. How Karnataka’s IT Department procures AI/software tools

### 4.1 Policy direction under IT Policy 2025–2030

Karnataka’s new IT Policy 2025–2030, approved by the Cabinet with an outlay of ₹967.12 crore, sets explicit goals around increasing the IT sector’s contribution to GSDP, tripling software exports, and driving large‑scale investments in AI and emerging technologies “Beyond Bengaluru”. The policy positions Karnataka as an “AI‑native” destination and part of a “Deeptech Decade”, indicating strong political backing for AI projects in government.[^36][^37]

The IT/BT Department is also running initiatives such as NIPUNA Karnataka, an industry‑driven skilling programme co‑created with companies like Google, Microsoft and NVIDIA, which leverages AI‑related platforms for workforce training. These initiatives suggest the department is comfortable procuring or partnering for AI platforms when aligned with strategic programmes.[^38][^39]

### 4.2 Concrete AI and digital initiatives

Recent reporting highlights several state‑level AI initiatives anchored by the IT/BT Department:

- A Global Centre for Artificial Intelligence in Bengaluru, approved with an investment of around ₹28 crore, implemented by the IT/BT Department to foster responsible AI development and technology governance.[^40]
- An AI Centre of Excellence with the World Economic Forum, to share trends, collaborate on research, and support responsible AI adoption.[^40]
- A comprehensive AI Workforce Impact Study under NIPUNA Karnataka to inform IT Policy 2025 and skilling interventions.[^41][^42]

While these articles do not detail the exact contracting patterns, they make clear that the department typically uses standard government mechanisms – budgeted schemes under the IT Policy, RFPs, MoUs with partners, and centres of excellence – rather than one‑off tool subscriptions.[^39][^40]

### 4.3 What this means for your product

Any AI tool you sell to the IT/BT Department is likely to be procured in one of three ways:

- As part of a **centre of excellence or pilot project** with co‑funding (requiring an MoU and possibly a limited tender or EoI).
- Via a **standard RFP** issued on Karnataka’s e‑procurement portal or GeM for AI‑enabled platforms under IT Policy 2025–2030 schemes.[^43][^18]
- As a component of a larger **system‑integration contract** where your SaaS is bundled by an SI responding to an RFP.

Positioning your offering as an enabler for IT Policy outcomes (e.g., Deeptech Decade, AI‑native governance, skilling of revenue officers on AI) will help align with how the department frames new procurements.[^36][^40]

## 5. Existing AI or digital tools in Karnataka land revenue offices

### 5.1 Long‑running digital systems: Bhoomi and integrations

Karnataka’s Bhoomi project, launched around 2000, digitised land records and created a statewide Land Records Management System covering millions of land parcels. Bhoomi manages RTCs (Record of Rights, Tenancy and Crops), mutations and land‑record updates through taluk‑level back offices and citizen kiosks across the state.[^44][^7][^45][^46]

Bhoomi has been integrated with other Revenue‑Department systems such as Bhooswadeena (land acquisition), the Kaveri property‑registration system and banking interfaces, providing workflow automation for mutations and land‑acquisition processes rather than AI‑based drafting. Case studies describe role‑based workflows where Tahsildars, Sheristedars and Village Accountants work within Bhoomi to process land‑record changes.[^47][^48][^49]

### 5.2 New AI‑enabled systems: Kaveri 3.0 and Land‑Stack

In the 2026 budget, Karnataka announced a major upgrade to Kaveri and Bhoomi: a ₹65‑crore Kaveri 3.0 AI‑enabled property registration platform and a ₹50‑crore Bhoomi 8.0 upgrade as part of a GIS‑based Karnataka Land‑Stack. Kaveri 3.0 is described as a fully AI‑enabled, paperless system offering remote property registration, AI‑driven verification and document drafting, with strong e‑KYC and facial‑recognition security for around 43 revenue services.[^8][^50][^51][^9]

Kaveri 3.0 will be integrated with Bhoomi 8.0, Mojini, e‑Swathu and e‑Asthi through the Land‑Stack, providing a unified digital view of land and property data and enabling automatic property mutation after registration. However, available descriptions focus on AI for verification and registration work‑flows, not on generating quasi‑judicial speaking orders by DDLR/Tahsildar.[^9][^52][^8]

### 5.3 Gap for AI‑assisted order drafting

There is no public evidence of a specialised AI system currently used within Karnataka’s DDLR or Tahsildar offices to draft quasi‑judicial revenue orders (conversion, phodi, encroachment, regularisation, etc.). Existing systems automate data capture, workflow routing and document templates, while judicial AI tools like Digital Courts 2.1 and LegRAA are being piloted within the judiciary rather than revenue administration.[^53][^47]

This creates a clear whitespace for an AI drafting assistant focused on land‑revenue orders that integrates with Bhoomi/Land‑Stack data but remains subject to human review and approval by revenue officers.

## 6. KSWAN and implications for your SaaS architecture

### 6.1 What KSWAN is

The Karnataka State Wide Area Network (KSWAN) is a secure MPLS‑based network set up in 2009 under the Centre for e‑Governance to provide LAN and WAN connectivity to government offices across the state. It connects thousands of government offices across departments in a multi‑tier architecture (state headquarters to districts, districts to taluks/blocks, and onwards to gram panchayats), providing a closed user group (CUG) network for data, voice and video.[^54][^55][^56][^57]

A recent KSWAN bandwidth RFP describes a three‑tier MPLS network connecting state, district and block points of presence, with specified SLAs on availability, latency and bandwidth, indicating that most departmental applications (including Bhoomi) are accessed over KSWAN from field offices.[^55][^47]

### 6.2 Does your SaaS need to “run inside” KSWAN?

By design, SWANs across India are meant to provide secure connectivity between government offices and state data centres, and can also act as the path to reach trusted external clouds via controlled gateways. MeitY’s MeghRaj framework and procurement guidelines envision departments consuming MeitY‑empanelled cloud services (IaaS/PaaS/SaaS) over secure networks – which, in Karnataka’s case, is primarily KSWAN.[^56][^11][^5]

For a drafting SaaS, there are three realistic deployment patterns:

- **Hosted in the Karnataka State Data Centre (SDC),** directly on the KSWAN intranet (no public internet exposure), accessed only from government IP ranges.
- **Hosted on a MeitY‑empanelled public cloud region in India,** reachable over the internet, with KSWAN providing secured outbound internet connectivity from government offices to your domain.[^5][^10]
- **Hybrid,** where sensitive components (databases, logs) sit in SDC/GCCloud while stateless API front‑ends run on MeitY‑empanelled public cloud.

Which model applies will depend on the specific RFP and CeG’s architecture preferences, but in all cases you should assume that your system must be reachable from within KSWAN, honour its IP whitelisting and security rules, and preferably keep all data within India.

## 7. Karnataka’s Responsible AI Committee and likely procurement guidelines

### 7.1 Mandate and timelines of the Committee

In March 2026, Karnataka constituted a high‑level Committee on Responsible Artificial Intelligence, chaired by Infosys co‑founder Kris Gopalakrishnan and co‑chaired by N. Manjula, Secretary of the Department of Electronics, IT, Biotechnology and Science & Technology. The committee’s mandate is to develop a Responsible AI policy and implementation roadmap for Karnataka to guide safe, ethical and transparent adoption of AI across government systems and citizen‑facing services.[^58][^59][^60][^61]

The official statements indicate that the committee will prepare a policy framework, risk‑classification system for AI applications used in governance, and implementation roadmap, and that it is expected to submit an interim report within 60 days and final recommendations within 90 days.[^6][^60]

### 7.2 Likely direction of procurement guidelines

While detailed procurement guidelines have not yet been published, public statements and India‑level AI governance documents suggest several themes that are likely to appear:

- **Risk‑based AI classification** (low, moderate, high risk) for government AI systems, similar to MeitY’s IndiaAI Governance Guidelines, with stricter procurement conditions (e.g., audits, sandboxing, approvals) for high‑risk systems affecting rights, benefits or entitlements.[^60][^34]
- **Mandatory human oversight** for high‑impact decisions – AI tools (like an order‑drafting engine) would be positioned as decision‑support, with final orders signed by human officers.[^61][^60]
- **Data protection and localisation requirements,** aligning with the DPDP Act and cloud policies (use of Indian data centres, control over model training on government data, clear data‑processing agreements).[^35][^5]
- **Transparency and accountability obligations,** including logging of prompts and outputs, version control for models, and mechanisms for explaining AI‑assisted decisions in plain language.[^34][^60]
- **Exclusions or restrictions** on certain AI uses such as social scoring, disproportionate surveillance, discriminatory profiling and fully automated decisions without meaningful human involvement.[^60][^61]

Designing your product and documentation to meet these likely expectations – even before the policy is formally notified – will make it easier to align with upcoming RFP language and committee recommendations.

## 8. Typical budget range for Tahsildar/DDLR offices to buy software

Public documents provide detailed project‑level allocations for statewide IT systems (e.g., ₹50 crore for Bhoomi 8.0, ₹65 crore for Kaveri 3.0), but do not break these down into annual software budgets per Tahsildar or DDLR office. Instead, software and IT systems for land records and registration are funded centrally under Revenue‑Department schemes and implemented through Bhoomi, Kaveri and related projects, with field offices acting mainly as users.[^7][^47][^8][^9]

The Karnataka Financial Code and contingent‑expenditure rules do mention fixed allowances of contingencies for taluk offices and emphasise that no expenditure can exceed budget grants without Government sanction, but they do not specify explicit rupee limits for software spending per office in publicly available extracts. In practice, this means that independent per‑office procurement of new software is rare and most significant IT spending is planned and budgeted at the department/directorate level.[^17][^16]

For your business planning, it is safer to assume that:

- **Revenue‑department software is funded as multi‑crore state projects**, not as small annual line‑items at each taluk office.[^8][^9]
- **Per‑office discretionary budgets are likely sufficient only for minor tools or supplies**, and not for recurring AI SaaS subscriptions that handle official land‑record or order data.[^16]

## 9. Can startups sell “informally” to officers who pay from pocket?

### 9.1 IT‑resource policies and security

MeitY’s “Policy on Use of IT Resources of Government of India”, which is intended to guide both Central and State governments, states that IT resources issued by government are to be used primarily for government‑related purposes in a lawful and ethical manner, with departments responsible for enforcing compliance. The policy empowers implementing agencies (typically NIC/CeG) to deactivate or remove any device or resource from the government network if it is deemed a security threat and requires that all access to the network and internet be controlled and monitored.[^1]

Taken together with cloud‑procurement guidelines, which require that cloud services consumed by departments be MeitY‑empanelled and procured through GeM, this strongly discourages ad‑hoc use of un‑contracted third‑party SaaS for official work, even if an officer is personally paying for the subscription.[^11][^4][^5]

### 9.2 Conduct and data‑protection risks

Using an unofficial SaaS tool for official case‑files could also raise issues under service rules (sharing official records with an external system without authorisation) and under data‑protection norms once DPDP implementation tightens, because there would be no formal data‑processing agreement between the Government (as data fiduciary) and the SaaS provider.[^34][^35]

While officers sometimes experiment with generic AI tools for drafting, there is no legal cover or explicit policy that would allow a department to rely on such drafts as part of an official system of record without going through formal procurement, security review and approvals.

**Actionable takeaway:** do not design your business model around officers personally paying for your SaaS to draft official orders. Treat any such usage, at most, as unpaid pilots or PoCs using synthetic or heavily redacted data, with a clear path towards formal departmental procurement once value has been demonstrated.

## 10. Indian SaaS startups that have sold to land/revenue offices – and their approach

### 10.1 Examples from other states

Direct examples of SaaS startups selling into Karnataka’s land‑revenue offices are not yet publicly documented; most large systems (Bhoomi, Kaveri, Bhooswadeena) were built by NIC and state agencies. However, there are relevant examples from other Indian states where startups or specialised vendors provide AI or digital platforms for land‑records work:[^44][^47]

- In West Bengal, Vixplor Analytics’ DocXplor – an AI‑powered ICR/OCR and RPA platform – is being used by the state’s Finance Department to digitise and analyse old land‑registration records, converting handwritten registers into structured digital chains of title.[^62]
- Drone‑as‑a‑Service vendors have been engaged by Karnataka and other states for large‑scale land‑survey projects under revenue departments, although specific startup names are often not mentioned in public orders.[^63][^64]

These cases show that state revenue departments are willing to work with relatively young technology companies when they solve a clear pain point (digitising legacy records, accelerating surveys) and can pass security and procurement scrutiny.

### 10.2 Common go‑to‑market patterns

From these examples and broader public‑procurement practice, several patterns emerge for selling SaaS or AI tools into land‑revenue ecosystems:

- **Start with a tightly scoped pilot** that addresses a politically visible pain point (e.g., backlog of mutation orders, delay in drafting conversion orders, inconsistencies in RTC narratives) and demonstrate clear time savings and error reduction.
- **Align with national schemes** such as DILRMP 2.0 or the India‑wide Land‑Stack initiative, so that your solution can be framed as a plug‑in module rather than a standalone experiment.[^65][^66]
- **Integrate with existing systems** (Bhoomi, Kaveri, Mojini, Land‑Stack) via APIs or secure batch exports, rather than proposing a parallel database.[^67][^47]
- **Use formal procurement routes** – EoIs, limited pilots funded as part of larger projects, and eventual RFPs – with state IT/Revenue departments, rather than “shadow IT” subscriptions.

For Karnataka specifically, the combination of Kaveri 3.0, Bhoomi 8.0 and the Land‑Stack, plus the new Responsible AI Committee, creates a timely opening to position your product as a specialised AI‑assisted drafting layer for quasi‑judicial orders that sits on top of the state’s core land‑data infrastructure.

## 11. Concrete next steps for your SaaS

Based on the 10 questions, a pragmatic roadmap for your product would include:

1. **Regulatory and infra alignment**
   - Choose a MeitY‑empanelled CSP and keep data in India, ready to demonstrate compliance.[^10][^5]
   - Build a KSWAN‑friendly deployment model (IP whitelisting, VPN/SDC option).[^54][^55]
2. **Market access setup**
   - Obtain DPIIT Startup Recognition and register on GeM, targeting relevant SaaS/AI categories and Startup Runway.[^12][^25]
   - Prepare a concise security and AI‑governance whitepaper mapping your system to IndiaAI and anticipated Karnataka Responsible‑AI principles.[^60][^34]
3. **Government‑facing product strategy**
   - Design integrations and demos around Bhoomi and Land‑Stack use‑cases (e.g., draft speaking orders based on RTC, mutation chain and mojini sketches), not generic legal drafting.[^7][^9]
   - Target pilots with the Revenue Department (Bhoomi Monitoring Cell, SSLR, Registration & Stamps) and the IT/BT Department’s AI/Deeptech initiatives, framed as supporting Kaveri 3.0/Bhoomi 8.0 outcomes.[^40][^8]
4. **Procurement pathway**
   - Propose limited‑scope PoCs under existing digitisation/AI budgets, with a clear plan to move into a formal RFP or GeM‑based tender after pilot success.
   - Avoid informal, officer‑paid subscriptions for official use; instead, use such relationships to co‑design the product and gather non‑sensitive training data and feedback.

If followed, this path keeps you compliant with Karnataka’s procurement and IT‑governance frameworks while positioning your SaaS as a natural extension of the state’s ongoing AI‑enabled land‑records reforms.
