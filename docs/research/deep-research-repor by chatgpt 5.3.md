# 1. Individual vs. Department Purchases  
In Karnataka, **officers cannot simply buy software on their own**; purchases must follow official procurement rules.  Under the *Karnataka Transparency in Public Procurements Act (1999)*, every “Procurement Entity” (e.g. a Tahsildar’s office) must use approved procedures (tenders, contracts or other rules) for goods and services【32†L432-L439】.  Section 5 of the Act explicitly prohibits procurement outside the tender process【32†L432-L439】.  The Act has since been amended to allow online procurement via GeM as a sanctioned channel【32†L485-L493】.  In practice, a Tahsildar or DDLR would have to use the department’s delegated budget and approved procurement portal (e.g. Karnataka’s e-Procurement or GeM) to buy software.  **No provision exists for an officer to bypass this**.  Even small purchases are typically made under delegated financial powers (with vouchers or e-payments) rather than personal payment. 

# 2. GeM Registration for Startups  
To sell to government bodies (including Karnataka), a startup must register on the Government e-Marketplace (GeM) platform.  The **GeM seller onboarding** requires standard KYC (PAN, Aadhaar, company incorporation proof, bank details, GST, etc.) and, for the GeM *Startup Runway*, a DPIIT startup-recognition number【27†L354-L362】.  In other words, a SaaS startup needs DPIIT certification (Startup India) plus the usual documents to create a seller profile on GeM【27†L354-L362】. Once registered, the startup can list its software/services for government buyers. 

Legally, while Karnataka’s rules (and GFR 149) encourage GeM use【32†L485-L493】, the platform is effectively **mandatory** if you want to sell via GeM.  In practice, all central and most state procurements go through GeM, so to reach those buyers you must be registered.  One analysis notes: “Any company seeking to provide goods or services to the government…has to be registered on GeM”【43†L366-L374】. (However, Karnataka does have its own rules; loosely, it uses GeM heavily but also publishes tenders on its state procurement portal.) In summary, the **GeM registration process** is online: prepare company docs (PAN, GST, etc.), get DPIIT startup recognition, and apply on gem.gov.in.  While startups outside GeM can theoretically respond to non-GeM tenders, for most state purchases GeM is the de facto channel, so registration is essential for government sales【43†L366-L374】【27†L354-L362】. 

# 3. Required Certifications/Approvals  
Key credentials for selling IT/AI products to government include:  

- **DPIIT Startup Recognition** – Required for GeM’s startup benefits【27†L354-L362】 and to access relaxed bidding criteria (no prior turnover/EMD) on GeM.  
- **STQC Certification** – Many government IT procurements mandate STQC (Standardization, Testing and Quality Certification) compliance for software and hardware (especially where security or interoperability are concerned).  While not specific to AI, STQC is often required for IT systems.  
- **MeitY Empanelment** – The Ministry of Electronics & IT empanels cloud/IT services providers (with STQC audit) for government use【12†L51-L59】.  If our SaaS uses cloud infrastructure, having it on the MeitY-approved list can be a strong selling point (and in some cases a requirement for PSU/state IT procurements)【12†L51-L59】.  
- **Other ISO/Security Standards** – Government IT purchases often look for ISO 27001 or equivalent security certifications (and soon compliance with India’s Digital Personal Data Protection rules).  For AI specifically, India currently has no separate statutory AI-certification, but responsible-AI guidelines may imply certain checks.  

There is no single AI-specific license yet; instead, ensure compliance with general IT norms (data privacy, cybersecurity, quality testing).  In summary, a startup should be DPIIT-recognized, consider STQC tests if applicable, and align with MeitY guidelines (especially for cloud services)【12†L51-L59】【27†L354-L362】.

# 4. Karnataka IT Dept. Procurement Practices  
Karnataka’s IT/BT Department (Minister Priyank Kharge) is actively engaging with AI but procures tools via formal projects.  For instance, in Feb 2026 the **State Cabinet approved a ₹67.20 Cr “Social Media Analytics Solution”** to monitor fake news, to be acquired by e-tender【65†L200-L208】【65†L234-L242】.  This AI system (SMAS) will be procured through standard tender committees, not via ad-hoc purchase. Similarly, large software systems are funded in the state budget (e.g. ₹50 Cr for Bhoomi 8.0 and ₹65 Cr for Kaveri 3.0 land record upgrades【93†L91-L99】).  There is also a new policy pushing departments to source ~5% of procurement from startups【51†L146-L154】, signaling openness to emerging products. In short, Karnataka procures software (including AI tools) through sanctioned tenders or GeM, often tied to budgetary schemes or state-funded digitization projects.  Recent examples: the SMAS tender above【65†L200-L208】【65†L234-L242】, and ongoing e-governance rollouts (e.g. integrated land database, automated mutations, etc.). Startups should monitor Karnataka’s e-Procurement and participate in these tenders or sector-specific RFPs.

# 6. KSWAN (Karnataka State Wide Area Network)  
**KSWAN** is Karnataka’s government-owned secure network connecting state, district and taluk offices【82†L814-L822】.  It provides MPLS/WAN connectivity (and Internet access) to ~7,000 government offices, carrying voice, video and data【82†L814-L822】.  In practice, KSWAN is the communication backbone for government offices. 

For our SaaS, KSWAN’s role is that of the network: officials in a taluk will use KSWAN to reach our service over the Internet.  We do **not** need to deploy the SaaS inside KSWAN, but our service must be accessible from it (i.e. not blocked by firewalls, use secure HTTPS, etc.).  If the tool were hosted on Karnataka’s own servers, it might need to reside in the data centers connected to KSWAN, but for a typical cloud SaaS, ensuring compatibility with government network security (e.g. certificates, no banned protocols) is usually enough.  In summary: **KSWAN is the highway** by which our tool will be accessed at government offices【82†L814-L822】.  We should ensure our SaaS can be reached over that network (and possibly whitelist IPs or work with ITD if needed), but the application itself need not be inside KSWAN.

# 7. Responsible AI Committee Guidelines (Procurement)  
Karnataka’s new Responsible AI Committee (chaired by Kris Gopalakrishnan, Mar 2026) will draft guidelines for AI use, including procurement.  Published reports say the panel will stress transparency, accountability and citizen trust in AI systems【87†L156-L164】.  We expect recommended **procurement rules** to reflect these principles: for example, RFPs may require independent fairness/security audits of AI tools, documentation of data and models, and proof of compliance with the Data Protection Act. The committee plans a “risk-classification” of AI use cases【88†L188-L196】, so high-risk applications (e.g. welfare delivery, policing) would undergo stricter procurement scrutiny (additional testing, oversight clauses) than low-risk ones.  They are explicitly tasked to “frame procurement guidelines and vendor due diligence frameworks”【87†L178-L184】【88†L208-L212】, which likely means vetting vendors for ethical practices and including penalty clauses. In short, expect procurement criteria to mandate that AI vendors show how their models avoid bias, protect privacy, and can be audited. Contracts may require open disclosure of algorithms, third-party audits, and alignment with national Responsible AI norms. The news confirms they will ban certain practices (e.g. social scoring)【88†L198-L207】 and will demand safety/ethics for critical use cases. All told, the Committee will push Karnataka to **only buy AI solutions that meet ethical, security and transparency standards**, with procurement specifications reflecting those safeguards【87†L156-L164】【88†L204-L212】.

# 9. Informal Purchase by an Officer  
No — officers should not and **do not normally** buy official software with personal money.  As noted, Karnataka procurement rules mandate formal purchasing channels【32†L432-L439】. Personal payments would violate transparency and financial norms. There is no official mechanism for an officer to reimburse themselves after an out-of-pocket purchase for office use.  Even for small items, purchase is done via petty cash vouchers or official credit, not by personal invoice. We found no allowance for “direct buy and reimburse” in any Karnataka procurement or financial rules.  In fact, the KTPP Act’s blanket requirement for tender/Gem procurement【32†L432-L439】 implies any purchase must be budgeted and approved; paying from pocket would break that rule. In short, an informal private sale to a government office is **not permitted** by the rules.

# 10. Startup Case Studies (State Land Offices)  
We found **no published case** of an Indian startup selling a SaaS drafting tool directly to a state revenue or land office.  Startups in the land-tech or AI space generally engage through formal routes. One recent example in land records tech is the YC-backed startup Landeed, which built a unified search over multiple states’ land records【100†L28-L36】, but its customers are mainly private (banks, builders), not a state government. In general, successful approaches for startups targeting state agencies include:  **(a)** Participating in state startup initiatives or innovation challenges (Karnataka’s ~5% procurement quota for startups【51†L146-L154】 encourages this), and **(b)** Registering on GeM and bidding for published tenders or issuing unsolicited proposals.  For example, Karnataka’s new policy explicitly encourages departments to procure from registered startups【51†L146-L154】.  However, we did not find a specific “startup sold AI tool to land office” story.  In lieu of a concrete case, the best guidance is to use the established channels (GeM, e-Tender, Startup Runway【27†L354-L362】) and to note how peers have won contracts: usually by responding to government RFPs or pilot programs rather than secret, informal deals.
