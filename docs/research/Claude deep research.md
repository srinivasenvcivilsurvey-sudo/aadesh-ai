# Selling AI SaaS to Karnataka revenue offices: a complete playbook

Karnataka's land revenue offices — from Tahsildar courts to the DDLR — draft thousands of legal orders manually each year, making them a compelling market for an AI drafting tool. But selling software to Indian state government is a labyrinth of procurement rules, certifications, and institutional gatekeepers. This report answers ten critical questions about how a SaaS startup can navigate this terrain, with Karnataka-specific detail as of April 2026.

The bottom line: **a sub-₹5 lakh annual subscription can bypass formal tendering** under Karnataka's amended procurement law, but even small purchases demand three quotations, proper documentation, and budget authority. The most viable path combines GeM registration, DPIIT Startup India recognition, and Karnataka's own KITS empanelment — then targets a district-level pilot sponsored by a Deputy Commissioner with discretionary IT funds, rather than attempting a statewide rollout from day one.

---

## 1. Officers can buy independently — but only below ₹5 lakh, and with paperwork

The question of whether a Tahsildar or DC can simply purchase your SaaS tool hinges on a single legislative threshold. The **Karnataka Transparency in Public Procurements (KTPP) Act, 1999** — amended most recently in **2023 and again in 2025** — exempts all procurement of goods and services valued at **₹5 lakh or below** from the formal tender process (Section 4(e)(i), post-2023 amendment). Before the 2023 amendment, the threshold for non-construction goods and services was just ₹1 lakh, making this a significant expansion of officer discretion.

However, exemption from KTPP does not mean exemption from all rules. Even below ₹5 lakh, the **Karnataka Financial Code (KFC), 1958** and the **Karnataka Stores Purchase Rules** require officers to obtain at least **three quotations** for comparison, maintain proper purchase records, ensure budget provision exists under the correct head of account, and secure sanction from the competent authority per their delegation of financial powers. Violating the KTPP Act carries penalties of up to **three years imprisonment and ₹5,000 fine** (Section 23), so officers are understandably cautious.

The hierarchy of financial delegation works as follows. The Karnataka Finance Department publishes a **Book of Financial Powers** that specifies monetary limits for each rank. While the exact current limits for each revenue officer rank were not accessible in full online (the document requires access via the Finance Department portal, much of which is in Kannada), the general framework is clear:

- **Deputy Commissioner (DC)**: Highest district-level authority with the broadest purchasing powers among revenue officers — typically authorized for purchases up to several lakhs independently for office needs
- **Assistant Commissioner (AC)**: Subordinate to DC, more limited independent purchasing authority
- **Tahsildar**: Head of taluk-level office with the most constrained budget — typically limited to routine office contingency expenditure
- **DDLR/ADLR**: State-level officers with departmental-level authority, but software procurement decisions for the department would typically flow through the Revenue Secretary and the Centre for e-Governance (CeG)

For **purchases above ₹5 lakh**, the full KTPP tender process kicks in — publication in the Tender Bulletin, formal bid evaluation, and procurement through the Karnataka e-Procurement Portal (eproc.karnataka.gov.in). Above **₹1 crore**, publication in the state-level Tender Bulletin becomes mandatory.

**Practical implication for pricing**: Structure your SaaS subscription at **₹4-5 lakh per year per office** (or lower) to remain below the KTPP threshold. This allows individual officers to procure through a simplified quotation process rather than a full tender.

## 2. GeM registration is free, fast, and increasingly essential

The **Government e-Marketplace (GeM)** is India's national procurement portal with over **22 lakh registered sellers** and approaching **₹6 lakh crore** in cumulative business. While GeM is centrally mandated for all Union government purchases under **Rule 149 of the General Financial Rules (GFR) 2017**, it is not technically mandatory for Karnataka state government entities — they follow the KTPP Act. However, Karnataka state departments **increasingly use GeM** alongside the state's own procurement system, and being listed there signals credibility.

**Registration process and requirements** are straightforward:

1. Visit gem.gov.in → Sign Up → Select "Seller" → Choose "Service Provider" (for SaaS)
2. PAN validation and Aadhaar OTP verification of authorized signatory
3. Complete business profile (company registration certificate, GST registration, bank account details with IFSC and cancelled cheque)
4. List your product/service under relevant UNSPSC categories — for AI SaaS, look at "Professional Outsourcing Service" or "PC Software" categories
5. GeM team verifies and approves listings

**Timeline**: 5–7 working days with complete documentation, up to 20 days for complex categories. **Cost**: Completely free — GeM **discontinued the caution money requirement in March 2026**, so there is no upfront deposit. **Documents needed**: PAN card, Aadhaar, GST registration, company incorporation certificate, and bank account details.

**GeM procurement thresholds** under GFR 2017 (applicable to central entities and increasingly referenced by state buyers):

| Purchase Value | Method |
|---|---|
| Up to ₹50,000 | Direct purchase, no quotations needed |
| ₹50,001 to ₹25 lakh | L1 purchase or direct purchase on GeM |
| Above ₹25 lakh | Formal bidding or e-Reverse Auction on GeM |

**DPIIT-recognized startups get significant advantages on GeM**: exemption from Earnest Money Deposit (EMD), relaxation of prior experience and turnover requirements, and access to the **Startup Runway** — a dedicated innovation section where government buyers can discover startup products. As of the February 2026 DPIIT framework update (G.S.R. 108(E)), AI tools qualify under the new **"Deep Tech" category** with a 20-year recognition window and a raised turnover ceiling of **₹300 crore**.

The most practical approach: register on GeM even if your initial sales go through direct quotation. GeM listing serves as a credibility marker, and some Karnataka officers may prefer purchasing through GeM for the audit trail it provides.

## 3. Four registrations matter most; the rest can wait

The certification landscape for selling AI software to Indian government can feel overwhelming, but a startup can prioritize ruthlessly. Here is what actually matters, ranked by urgency:

**Tier 1 — Do immediately (free or low-cost, essential for any government sale):**

- **DPIIT Startup India Recognition**: Free, processed in 2–7 days through the National Single Window System (nsws.gov.in). Provides EMD exemption, prior-experience waiver, and access to GeM Startup Runway. Eligibility: entity less than 10 years old, annual turnover below ₹200 crore (₹300 crore for deep tech).
- **GeM Registration**: Free, 5–7 days as described above.
- **KITS Registration (Karnataka-specific)**: Registration with the **Karnataka Innovation and Technology Society** under the Department of Electronics, IT, Bt and S&T is effectively mandatory for startups seeking Karnataka government procurement. Apply through k-tech.karnataka.gov.in. This enables empanelment that allows **limited tenders up to ₹50 lakhs** with EMD elimination. Empanelment is valid for 3 years with a cap of 2 contracts per procurement entity per year. **KEONICS** issued an Expression of Interest in FY 2025-26 for "Empanelment of ICT Services Startups and MSMEs."

**Tier 2 — Do within 3–6 months (builds credibility, may be required in tenders):**

- **ISO 27001:2022 (Information Security Management)**: Not legally mandatory but **effectively required** for most government tenders. Cost: ₹2–10 lakh depending on organization size. Timeline: 3–6 months for implementation and audit. India's IT Act Section 43A explicitly references ISO 27001 as the standard for "reasonable security practices."

**Tier 3 — Address as needed (not required upfront):**

- **STQC Certification**: Only mandatory for specific hardware categories. For software, STQC provides third-party quality audits mainly for large e-governance projects. Cost: ₹25,000 to ₹10 lakh. MSMEs may receive **50% fee rebates**. Not needed for initial small-scale deployments.
- **MeitY Cloud Empanelment**: You don't need this yourself — just **host your SaaS on an already-empanelled cloud provider** (AWS Mumbai/Hyderabad, Azure India, or Google Cloud Mumbai/Delhi). This satisfies the MeghRaj (GI Cloud) requirement that government data be stored within India.
- **SOC 2, CMMI**: Rarely required in Indian government procurement. Nice to have but low priority.

**AI-specific governance**: India has not enacted a standalone AI law. The **India AI Governance Guidelines** (November 2025) are principle-based, covering seven "sutras" — trust, safety, fairness, accountability, transparency, understandability, and human-centric values. These are not legally binding requirements but increasingly referenced in government AI procurement criteria. Karnataka's Responsible AI Committee (see Question 7) will likely formalize these into state-level procurement requirements by mid-2026.

**Data localization is non-negotiable**: All data processing and storage for government clients must occur within India. Ensure your cloud infrastructure is India-based.

## 4. Karnataka is investing heavily in AI — but procurement still flows through established channels

Karnataka has positioned itself as India's AI capital with aggressive policy and budgetary commitments under **IT Minister Priyank Kharge** and **IT Secretary Dr. N. Manjula, IAS**. The **Karnataka IT Policy 2025-2030**, unveiled at the Bengaluru Tech Summit in November 2025, prioritizes AI integration across government services.

Key budget allocations signal serious intent: **₹50 crore over five years** for the Centre for Applied AI for Tech Solutions (CATS), **₹16 crore** for two AI Centres of Excellence in Bengaluru (in collaboration with IBAB, C-CAMP, and NASSCOM), **₹5 crore** for an AI Centre of Excellence at IIIT Raichur, the **BRAINz** (Bangalore Robotics and AI Innovation Zone) campus under ARTPARK/IISc with ISRO and KEONICS, and a **₹400 crore** AI and deep-tech push announced at BTS 2025. The 2026-27 budget also allocated **₹2 crore for an AI-based smart court system** — directly relevant to AI-assisted legal drafting.

The procurement ecosystem for AI tools flows through several institutions. **KEONICS** (Karnataka State Electronics Development Corporation) is the primary IT procurement vehicle for state departments. The **Centre for e-Governance (CeG)** under DPAR steers e-governance policy and manages core infrastructure. **NIC Karnataka** implements national and state-level IT projects and has historically built most revenue department systems. **KDEM** (Karnataka Digital Economy Mission) serves as the bridge between government and industry, with CEO Sanjeev Kumar Gupta leading efforts to position Karnataka as an AI leader.

Recent partnership activity is extensive: a **Letter of Intent with the World Economic Forum** (February 2024) to establish WEF's sole dedicated AI hub in India; collaborations with **ARTPARK at IISc** for robotics and AI research; an MoU between **KDEM and STPI** (March 2026) for startup support; and the **Karnataka Innovation Authority (KIA) Act, 2020** now being amended to create a regulatory sandbox specifically for AI. IT Secretary Dr. Manjula confirmed at the India Digital Summit (January 2026) that Karnataka is framing a regulatory sandbox under the KIA Act for AI and emerging technologies, with exemptions for up to 2 years.

For a startup, this means the **political and policy environment is favorable** — Karnataka's leadership actively wants to deploy AI in government. But the procurement machinery still runs through KEONICS, CeG, and established channels. The most practical entry point is through the **KITS empanelment** process and targeting specific department-level pilots.

## 5. No AI tool exists for order drafting — this is a genuine whitespace

Karnataka's revenue offices operate an interconnected suite of digital systems, but **none of them include AI-assisted legal order drafting**. This represents a clear market gap.

The existing digital ecosystem includes five major platforms. **Bhoomi** (launched 2000) manages rural land records across all 176+ taluks — RTCs, mutations, and sketches — but is purely a database and records system. **Kaveri** handles property registration, stamp duty calculation, and e-stamping for the Registration & Stamps Department. **UPOR** (Urban Property Ownership Records) creates certified property ownership records for 213 Urban Local Bodies — the urban equivalent of Bhoomi. **Dishaank**, developed by KSRSAC under the K-GIS program, is a GPS-enabled mobile app for land identification overlaying cadastral maps on satellite imagery with Bhoomi integration — useful for field surveys but not document creation. **RCCMS** (Revenue Court Case Monitoring System), developed by NIC, is the most relevant system — it tracks the full lifecycle of revenue court cases from filing through hearings to disposal across DC, AC, and Tahsildar courts. RCCMS handles case entry, proceedings recording, cause lists, and document uploads, but **it does not draft orders**.

The current workflow for legal order drafting in revenue courts is **manual or semi-digital**. Officers draft orders based on Karnataka Land Revenue Act, 1964 formats, referencing case records in RCCMS, land records in Bhoomi/UPOR, and property details in Kaveri. The drafting itself happens in word processors or sometimes by hand, with no AI assistance. While commercial AI legal drafting tools exist in India (Supreme Today AI, LegalFly), **none are deployed in Karnataka revenue offices**.

NIC Karnataka has developed some AI solutions — face authentication for transport, blockchain for land records — but no AI order-drafting capability for revenue courts. The **₹2 crore allocation for an AI-based smart court system** in Karnataka's 2026-27 budget suggests the government recognizes this gap, but no specific product has been identified or procured yet.

This whitespace is your strongest selling proposition. An AI tool that integrates with RCCMS case data, references Bhoomi/UPOR records, and generates draft orders in the correct legal format under the Karnataka Land Revenue Act would address a genuine unmet need with no incumbent competitor.

## 6. KSWAN connects 7,000+ offices but cloud SaaS can work over it

The **Karnataka State Wide Area Network (KSWAN)** is the backbone connecting all government offices across the state, managed by the **Centre for e-Governance (CeG)** under DPAR. It provides MPLS/P2P-based WAN connectivity to over **7,000 government offices across 72+ departments**, spanning State, District, and Taluk levels with **99.9% uptime** targets and redundant connections.

The network is secured by a **Fortinet Security Fabric** — FortiGate Next-Generation Firewalls manage centralized internet traffic with consistent security policies across the network and multi-cloud environments. NIC's Network Security Division provides 24×7 monitoring, security audits, and manages security devices across the network.

**A cloud-based SaaS tool can work over KSWAN** — it does not need to be installed on-premises. KSWAN provides both LAN and WAN connectivity with internet access (via Internet Leased Line with failover). The existing systems like Bhoomi and Kaveri are web-based applications accessed through standard browsers, demonstrating that web-based tools are already deployed and functional. The technical requirements for compatibility are:

- **Browsers**: Chrome v87.0+, Microsoft Edge v87.0+, Firefox v83.0+ (per Karnataka government website standards)
- **Operating System**: Windows-based endpoints (NIC standard, managing 2 lakh+ endpoints nationally)
- **Data hosting**: Must be on India-based, MeitY-empanelled cloud infrastructure
- **Key constraint**: Bandwidth at taluk-level offices may be limited compared to district or state headquarters. Design your application to work on modest bandwidth — lightweight page loads, minimal large file transfers, and ideally some offline/low-connectivity capability

The **NIC Karnataka State Data Centre** provides abundant compute and storage, and Karnataka operates a **NIC Mini Cloud** setup. While your SaaS doesn't need to be hosted on NIC infrastructure, if it handles sensitive government data, hosting on an empanelled CSP (AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud's India regions) is effectively required. The practical approach: build a standard web application that works in Chrome on Windows over a decent broadband connection, ensure India-based hosting, and you'll be technically compatible with KSWAN office environments.

## 7. The Responsible AI Committee will shape procurement rules — but hasn't published them yet

Karnataka formed its **Responsible AI Committee on March 12, 2026**, making it one of the first Indian states to create a dedicated body for AI governance. Chaired by **Kris Gopalakrishnan** (Infosys co-founder and Axilor Ventures chairman) and co-chaired by **Dr. N. Manjula, IAS** (IT Secretary), the committee includes heavy-hitters from Accenture, Kyndryl, IBM, Wipro, NASSCOM, IIIT Bangalore, Sarvam AI, and Trilegal.

The committee's mandate spans ten focus areas, but the most directly relevant for SaaS vendors is its explicit task of developing **Responsible AI Procurement Guidelines** for government departments. These will include vendor due diligence frameworks, transparency requirements regarding training data, and audit rights for AI systems procured by government. The committee is also developing a **risk classification framework** that will categorize AI systems by potential impact — determining different procurement requirements for different risk levels.

Key positions the committee has already discussed include prohibiting social scoring of citizens, unlawful surveillance, and discriminatory profiling; requiring meaningful **human oversight for high-stakes automated decision-making** (directly relevant to AI-drafted legal orders); and mandating safeguards for AI applications in welfare delivery, healthcare, education, policing, and financial decision-making.

**No published reports exist yet** as of April 2026 — the committee's **interim report is due around mid-May 2026**, and final recommendations by **mid-June 2026**. However, a startup can anticipate likely requirements based on the committee's stated focus: explainability of AI outputs, bias mitigation documentation, human-in-the-loop design (the officer reviews and approves all AI-drafted orders), data governance policies covering quality and provenance, and transparency about training data sources.

**Strategic implication**: Design your AI tool to be "Responsible AI Committee-ready" from day one. Build in explainability features (showing why specific legal language was suggested), maintain human-in-the-loop architecture (AI drafts, officer edits and approves), document your training data sources, and prepare for audit requirements. Being among the first AI tools to comply with Karnataka's Responsible AI framework — once published — would be a significant competitive advantage.

## 8. Taluk-level IT budgets are minimal — target the district or state level for funding

Detailed IT budget data for individual Tahsildar offices is not published in accessible state budget documents. However, research across budget analyses and procurement patterns reveals a clear picture: **taluk-level offices have extremely modest discretionary IT budgets**, while meaningful technology investment flows from the state or department level.

Karnataka's total budget for FY 2026-27 is **₹4,48,004 crore**, with 58–59% of revenue receipts consumed by committed expenditure (salaries, pensions, interest). The Revenue Department's detailed demand-for-grants — which would contain line-item IT allocations — is published in the full budget volumes on finance.karnataka.gov.in (primarily in Kannada) but is not broken out in summary budget analyses.

What is known about IT spending patterns in revenue offices: most technology systems (Bhoomi, Kaveri, RCCMS, UPOR) are **centrally developed and maintained by NIC or KEONICS** — individual offices don't fund their development. Taluk-level procurement tenders visible on procurement portals cover IT equipment basics — PCs, printers, scanners, CCTV — suggesting office contingency budgets of **a few lakhs per year for all office expenses combined**, not specifically for software. The "office contingency" or "permanent advance" (imprest) given to Tahsildars covers urgent routine expenses and is not designed for software subscriptions.

**State-level technology allocations** are far more substantial: ₹50 crore for CATS, ₹16 crore for AI CoEs, ₹2 crore for the AI smart court system, plus ₹300 crore Fund-of-Funds for startups and ₹100 crore deep-tech corpus. These funds flow through KEONICS, CeG, and department-level budgets — not through individual taluk offices.

**Pricing and funding strategy**: Rather than expecting each Tahsildar to fund the subscription from office contingency, pursue one of these approaches:

- **Department-level procurement**: Get the Revenue Department (through CeG or KEONICS) to procure your tool for deployment across multiple offices, funded from the department's IT budget head
- **District-level pilot**: Target a progressive DC who can allocate ₹3–5 lakh from district IT funds for a pilot across that district's revenue offices
- **Leverage state AI budgets**: Position your tool under the ₹2 crore AI smart court allocation or the CATS initiative
- **CSR or grant funding**: For pilots, explore corporate CSR partnerships or KDEM startup support programs

## 9. Personal purchases are off-limits, but creative pilot paths exist

An individual officer **cannot legally pay from personal funds** for software used in official government work — this creates accountability issues, questions about data ownership, and potential conflicts of interest. Government financial rules require that all official expenditure flow through sanctioned budget heads with proper documentation.

Using **office contingency funds** for software subscriptions is technically possible but practically constrained. Contingency expenditure must be recorded in a Contingent Register with the Head of Office's initials, charged against appropriate Object Heads, and meet the three-quotation requirement. Recurring SaaS subscriptions don't fit neatly into contingency frameworks designed for one-time purchases of office supplies. An officer who buys software this way assumes personal audit risk — if a later audit questions the purchase, the officer may face scrutiny.

That said, there are **legitimate paths to early adoption before formal statewide procurement**:

- **Free trial/freemium model**: Offer a genuinely free tier with limited functionality. Officers can use free software without procurement implications — no public money is spent. This builds familiarity and creates demand from the ground up. Many successful GovTech companies in India have used this approach.
- **Pilot under KTPP exemption**: A purchase below ₹5 lakh is exempt from formal KTPP tendering. If a DC or AC authorizes a pilot subscription with three quotations and proper documentation, this is legally sound.
- **KITS empanelment pilot**: Once empanelled through KITS, Karnataka's startup procurement framework allows limited tenders up to ₹50 lakh with simplified processes — designed exactly for this kind of pilot engagement.
- **Innovation sandbox**: The Karnataka Innovation Authority's regulatory sandbox (being expanded for AI) could provide a formal framework for testing your tool in government offices without full procurement.
- **CSR-funded deployment**: Partner with a corporate CSR program to fund deployment in a few offices, removing the procurement burden entirely from the government side.

**The risk to avoid**: any arrangement where money changes hands without proper documentation, budget sanction, and audit trail. Even well-intentioned informal purchases can create serious problems for the officer during audits. The KTPP Act explicitly states that **exemption proposals cannot retroactively regularize procurement already done without following proper procedures**.

## 10. Successful GovTech startups avoid the procurement maze — they find workarounds

Direct SaaS sales to Indian state revenue offices remain rare because most land records technology (Bhoomi, Dharani in Telangana, Meebhoomi in Andhra Pradesh) is built by **NIC or state IT agencies**, not commercial startups. However, several Indian GovTech companies offer instructive models for how to approach this market.

**Haqdarshak** (Bengaluru, founded 2016) built a platform connecting citizens with 5,000+ government welfare schemes across 14 languages. Rather than selling directly to government through procurement, they adopted a **B2G2C model** — partnering with governments and corporates to reach beneficiaries, with revenue coming primarily from institutional partnerships. They unlocked **$2.2 billion in government benefits for 7.6+ million families** and raised ₹1 crore on Shark Tank India. The lesson: sometimes the smartest path to government adoption is to wrap around government processes rather than sell directly into them.

**Qkopy/GoK-Direct** (founded 2018) achieved a true **SaaS B2G model** with the Kerala government, providing a mobile broadcasting platform for government-to-citizen communication during floods, Nipah outbreaks, and COVID-19. The government pays ongoing subscription fees. This succeeded because the tool addressed an urgent, visible need (emergency communication) with clear political returns for the government.

**eMudhra** (Bengaluru, founded 2008) sells digital signature certificates and PKI infrastructure directly to government departments — now publicly listed. Their success came from providing infrastructure that government mandated for itself (digital signatures for e-governance), making procurement a necessity rather than a discretionary purchase.

**CivicDataLab** (founded 2017) built open-source data platforms for government (Open Budgets India, JusticeHub) funded through **grants and development-sector partnerships** rather than government procurement. Their Assam Public Procurement Explorer and Health Procurement Performance Index were funded externally but deployed in government contexts.

**Patterns that work for a Karnataka revenue AI tool:**

- **Start with a free or freemium version** that individual officers can try without any procurement — this creates bottom-up demand and collects testimonials
- **Target a progressive DC for a paid district pilot** under the ₹5 lakh KTPP exemption, with proper quotation process and documentation
- **Use that pilot as social proof** to approach CeG or KEONICS for department-level procurement
- **Leverage Karnataka's AI moment**: With the IT Minister, AI Committee, regulatory sandbox, and ₹2 crore smart court budget, the political environment is uniquely favorable in 2026
- **Get KITS-empanelled** early — Karnataka's startup procurement framework with ₹50 lakh limited tenders is specifically designed for this scenario
- **Consider hybrid funding**: Use CSR, grant, or KDEM startup program support for initial deployment, transitioning to government-funded procurement once you have proven results
- **Build for RCCMS integration**: The biggest technical moat would be integration with NIC's RCCMS (Revenue Court Case Monitoring System), which already tracks all case data — an AI drafting tool that pulls case context from RCCMS would be dramatically more useful than a standalone product

The typical timeline from concept to first government payment in India's GovTech space is **12–24 months**. Plan accordingly with sufficient runway, and expect the sales cycle to be relationship-intensive — in-person demos at government offices consistently outperform email outreach.

## Conclusion

Karnataka in 2026 presents a rare alignment of opportunity for an AI legal drafting tool targeting revenue offices. The political leadership is actively championing AI adoption, the Responsible AI Committee is creating a governance framework (not blocking innovation), no competing AI tool exists in this space, and the RCCMS digital court system provides a natural integration point. The **₹5 lakh KTPP exemption threshold** creates a viable entry price point for office-level pilots without formal tendering.

The recommended sequence is: secure DPIIT recognition and KITS empanelment (weeks), register on GeM (days), offer a free trial version to build grassroots adoption, pursue a district-level pilot under simplified procurement, then leverage that pilot into a department-wide CeG/KEONICS procurement. Begin ISO 27001 certification in parallel. Design the product to be Responsible AI Committee-compliant from launch — human-in-the-loop, explainable outputs, India-hosted data, and audit-ready documentation. The startups that have succeeded in Indian GovTech didn't just build good software — they designed their go-to-market around the procurement system's constraints rather than fighting against them.
